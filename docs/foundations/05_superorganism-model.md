# 05 Superorganism Model

## Working Definition

The superorganism is emergent system-level behavior where distributed agents collectively optimize short-term throughput growth, often at long-term resilience cost.
In this project, superorganism emergence is explicitly conditioned by group-size scaling and historical subsistence transitions.

## Hypothesized Mechanisms

1. Money-price signals proxy for immediate resource access.
2. Competition selects for growth-maximizing organizations.
3. States stabilize growth via debt, infrastructure, and coercive order.
4. Network effects synchronize behavior across regions/sectors.
5. Group-size growth drives a shift from relational coordination to formal hierarchy.
6. Sedentism and agriculture increase storage/surplus, enabling larger institutions and persistent inequality.
7. **Oxytocin-driven tribalism**: in-group bonding promotes internal cooperation but amplifies out-group hostility. Cooperation produces affinity convergence (cultural assimilation), while conflict produces divergence (polarization). This creates a lock-in effect — once tribal boundaries solidify, cross-group coordination requires overcoming the othering penalty, making the superorganism pathway (hierarchical integration via coercion) more likely than voluntary federation.
8. **Governance stress cascades**: resource scarcity → price pressure → legitimacy erosion → extractive governance → further degradation. This positive feedback loop explains why civilizational overshoot often produces authoritarian consolidation rather than cooperative retrenchment.

## Observable Signatures

- Rising total throughput despite local warnings.
- Increasing dependence on fragile long supply chains.
- Policy preference for stability now over resilience later.
- Public narratives lagging biophysical constraints.
- Group-size-dependent increases in hierarchy and coercive governance.
- Regime transition fingerprints: mobility decline, surplus storage rise, centralization rise.
- Sedentarism fingerprints: storage dependence, property lock-in, labor specialization, and formalized institutions.
- Affinity clustering: formation of distinct cultural in-groups with high internal cooperation and elevated inter-group conflict.
- Governance policy cycling: laissez-faire during abundance → redistributive under moderate stress → extractive under crisis.
- Legitimacy erosion: declining public trust correlating with rising price pressure and ecological degradation.

## Experimental Design

Ablation tests:

1. Remove status drive.
2. Cap extraction strictly.
3. Increase ecological cost internalization.
4. Increase distributed governance.
5. Reduce network coupling.
6. Flatten affinity vectors (remove tribal dynamics) — does superorganism emerge without othering?
7. Lock governance to Redistributive — can the extraction spiral be prevented?
8. Start with homogeneous affinity (single tribe) — does cooperation-driven convergence prevent fragmentation?

Measure changes in:

- overshoot probability,
- inequality trajectory,
- coercion index,
- collapse/recovery dynamics.
